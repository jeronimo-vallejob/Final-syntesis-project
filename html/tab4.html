The nucleosynthesis of the Big Bang

The Big Bang nucleosynthesis is the process by which the nuclei of the lightest chemical elements, such as hydrogen, helium, and lithium, were formed during the first few minutes after the Big Bang.

In the early moments after the Big Bang, the universe was extremely hot and dense. In this environment, the nuclei of hydrogen and helium were formed through the fusion of protons and neutrons. The nuclei of lithium were also formed, although in much smaller quantities.

The Big Bang nucleosynthesis was a crucial process in the formation of the universe as we know it today, as it was the first time that elements heavier than hydrogen and helium were formed. These light elements are the building blocks of stars and galaxies.

The Big Bang nucleosynthesis occurred during a short period of time, approximately between one second and a few minutes after the Big Bang. After this period, the temperature and density of the universe decreased enough for nuclear fusion processes to stop and existing nuclei began to combine to form atoms.
