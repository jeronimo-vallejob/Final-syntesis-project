Cosmic Microwave Background

The cosmic microwave background radiation is a radiation that fills the entire universe and originated approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang. This radiation has a very precise spectral distribution, known as the blackbody spectrum, which corresponds to the current temperature of the universe (2.725 K). The existence of the cosmic microwave background radiation is one of the most solid pieces of evidence that the universe underwent a large initial explosion (the Big Bang) and has been expanding since then. The cosmic microwave background is one of the strongest pieces of evidence for the Big Bang because its existence and properties match exactly what is expected if the universe began with a big explosion approximately 13.8 billion years ago. Moreover, the small fluctuations in the temperature of the cosmic microwave background provide information about the distribution of matter in the early universe, which is also consistent with the Big Bang model.

The image we see of the cosmic microwave background shows small variations in the temperature of this radiation in different regions of the sky. These variations are key to understanding how galaxies and structures in the universe formed as we know it today. Therefore, the cosmic microwave background image is a window into the past that allows us to better understand the evolution of the universe

The Spherical Harmonics Decomposition

The Spherical Harmonics Decomposition is a mathematical technique used to analyze cosmic microwave background data, which is an electromagnetic radiation that fills the entire universe and originated from the Big Bang. Scientists from the Planck project have used this technique to analyze fluctuations in the temperature of this radiation and decompose them into different angular scales. The idea behind this technique is that fluctuations in the temperature of the cosmic microwave background are related to fluctuations in the density of the early universe, and these fluctuations can be expressed as a series of mathematical functions called spherical harmonics. By decomposing temperature patterns into different angular scales, scientists can study how large-scale structures of the universe formed, which in turn provides additional evidence for the Big Bang cosmological model.
